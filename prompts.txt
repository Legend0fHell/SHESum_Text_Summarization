# Structure-Aware Non-LLM Framework for Long and Multi-Document Summarization

## Executive judgment

Your overall design is **directionally strong**. The best parts are the structure-aware segmentation, the explicit extraction layer for support evidence, the separation between chunk-level processing and higher-level topic summarization, and the attempt to keep salient evidence grounded in source text instead of letting every higher layer depend only on previously generated summaries. That direction is well aligned with recent long-document work showing that hierarchical segmentation can help manage long inputs, while **context-aware support evidence** can improve factuality during hierarchical summarization. At the same time, recent work also shows that **naive hierarchical merging can hurt faithfulness**, and that long-context models display a **U-shaped positional bias**, often under-attending to material in the middle of long inputs. In other words, your idea is good, but it will work best only if each upper layer keeps access to source-linked evidence, not just lower-layer summaries. 

My short answer to your first question is therefore: **yes, with modifications**. The pipeline can be efficient and relatively non-token-intensive because most of the expensive work is moved to embeddings, clustering, and rule-based NLP rather than repeated LLM calls. Sentence-Transformers are designed for efficient embedding computation and fast similarity search, and spaCy is explicitly designed for production-scale NLP with fast NER, parsing, and rule-based components. The risk is not the indexing stage; the risk is the **summary stage** if you duplicate information by feeding the LLM full chunk text, salient sentences, and entity lists all at once in a redundant way. 

The most important architectural conclusion is this: treat the system as **a hierarchical evidence graph**, not just a hierarchical summary graph. Recent Findings ACL work on context-aware hierarchical merging reports that **support-style methods**, especially those paired with extractive selection of relevant input, outperform simpler hierarchical baselines on reference-based quality metrics, while replace-style methods can become overly specific and miss broader coverage. Recent NAACL work on positional bias further finds that changing input structure through hierarchical merging or incremental updating can itself hurt faithfulness. As an engineering inference from those results, every topic node in your graph should carry forward **source sentence IDs, chunk IDs, and evidence pointers**, not merely generated text and embeddings. 

## What in your flow is right and what I would change

Using sentence splitting first and then building **local windows of adjacent sentences** is a sound idea. Classical text segmentation methods such as TextTiling and C99 do not rely on isolated sentences; they rely on **local context windows** and boundary signals derived from adjacent blocks or ranked local similarity, precisely because topic shifts are easier to detect from smoothed local context than from one sentence at a time. That means your “three consecutive sentences” heuristic is defensible as a **boundary-detection representation**. 

I would, however, make one important change: use the three-sentence windows mainly for **boundary detection and local semantic smoothing**, but let the graph’s durable fine-grained evidence unit remain the **sentence**, optionally with an atomic-fact view for long complex sentences. Your summarization support layer wants source-grounded evidence that is precise enough to survive multiple summarization rounds. Long-document factuality evaluation work increasingly emphasizes sentence-level and fine-grained continuous scoring, and recent long-document evaluation studies show that sentence-level capabilities transfer meaningfully to summary-level evaluation when the setup is designed well. That strongly favors keeping sentence-level provenance alive throughout the graph. 

Your chunk graph with three relation factors—position, entities, and content—is also strong. The main adjustment I would make is to keep **positional weight low and adaptive**, not fixed. Your instinct that positionality should matter “to some extent” is correct. Work on long-form summarization shows strong positional bias, especially against middle content, so over-weighting position can worsen the exact failure mode you are trying to avoid. At the same time, work on long-document extractive summarization found that **local topic context** matters a great deal, in some settings even more than global context. The practical inference is that a mild position prior is useful within a document, but it should mostly act as a **tie-breaker or regularizer**, not as a dominant signal. In practice I would usually start with something like a small within-document prior, then tune it by domain. 

Leiden is a good clustering choice for chunk communities. The original Leiden paper reports that it is faster than Louvain and gives better, well-connected partitions, and the resolution parameter has a clear interpretation: higher resolution yields more, smaller communities; lower resolution yields fewer, larger ones. For your use case, that matters because you want a hierarchy whose granularity can be changed by user preference. I would therefore use Leiden with an explicit resolution schedule across layers instead of a single fixed setting. 

The last major change is computational rather than conceptual: do **not** build a dense all-pairs chunk graph once chunk count grows. Your design remains efficient only if you build a **sparse weighted graph**, typically from approximate nearest neighbors or top-k semantic neighbors plus local adjacency edges and entity-overlap edges. That keeps clustering linear-ish in practice instead of letting graph construction dominate runtime. This is an engineering inference, but it is consistent with how Sentence-Transformers are intended to be used for fast semantic search, clustering, and community detection over large embedding sets. 

## Salient sentence extraction without LLMs

For your second question, the **best practical non-LLM approach is not simple “sentence vs. whole-text embedding similarity, then top-k.”** That baseline is easy and sometimes acceptable, but by itself it tends to over-select generic central sentences, keep paraphrastic duplicates, and miss minority or contrastive viewpoints. A better design is a **hybrid salience selector** combining centrality, relevance, novelty control, and factual cues. The literature behind that recommendation is mature: LexRank and TextRank rank sentences by graph centrality, MMR explicitly balances relevance against redundancy, and submodular optimization gives a principled way to maximize coverage and diversity under a length budget for multi-document summarization. 

The strongest LLM-free recipe for your setting is a **two-stage selector**. First, score each candidate sentence within a chunk or within a topic cluster using a mixture of: semantic centrality within its local graph; relevance to the parent chunk/topic centroid; an entity-and-number bonus; a discourse bonus for contrast or concession markers; and a light structural bonus if the sentence is near a heading, in a lead sentence position inside a paragraph, or repeated across documents. Then, in the second stage, choose sentences with **MMR or a submodular greedy selector** so that every new sentence adds information instead of repeating what has already been selected. This is the most reliable way to reduce duplicates and near-duplicates **without** collapsing into genericity. 

A practical scoring function for each candidate sentence can be written as an engineering synthesis of those methods:

\[
\text{Score}(s)=
w_\text{cent}\cdot \text{Centrality}(s)+
w_\text{rel}\cdot \cos(s,\text{topic centroid})+
w_\text{ent}\cdot \text{EntityBonus}(s)+
w_\text{num}\cdot \text{FactBonus}(s)+
w_\text{disc}\cdot \text{ContrastBonus}(s)
\]

and then selection can use:

\[
\text{MMR}(s)=
\lambda \cdot \text{Score}(s)-
(1-\lambda)\cdot \max_{t\in S_\text{selected}}\cos(s,t)
\]

This is not a direct quote from any one paper; it is the most practical synthesis of centrality-based ranking, diversity-aware reranking, and structured evidence retention for your pipeline. Its advantage is that it directly addresses the three failure modes you named: it suppresses duplicates, penalizes unrelated sentences, and still keeps “small but important” contrastive ideas if you give them explicit discourse or entity-based support. The motivation for explicitly preserving support evidence is also consistent with recent context-aware hierarchical merging results. 

To preserve **contrastive ideas**, I would not trust embedding centrality alone. Contrastive sentences are often semantically “off-center” precisely because they represent exceptions, disagreements, trade-offs, or minority findings. A cheap and effective fix is to add a **contrast bonus** when a sentence contains reliable discourse cues such as contrast or concession markers, and a **minority-cluster rescue rule** that forces at least one high-quality sentence from each sufficiently large subcommunity inside a topic cluster. That recommendation is supported by discourse-marker work showing that contrast relations have reliable markers, and by multi-document summarization work on debatable or balanced summaries, which shows that a structured content plan is important when multiple perspectives must be preserved. 

For duplicate suppression, I would add a final **paraphrase-mining pass** over the shortlist of selected sentences. Sentence-Transformers provides paraphrase mining specifically for detecting semantically similar sentence pairs, and the same library’s extractive summarization example uses sentence embeddings plus LexRank to build extractive summaries. Together, those tools make a very practical CPU-friendly pipeline: embeddings for candidate scoring, graph centrality for salience, paraphrase mining for deduplication. 

If you are willing to move beyond pure unsupervised methods but still avoid LLM calls, the best next step is a **small extractive model or reranker**. BERTSUM showed that pretrained encoders can be very effective for extractive summarization, MatchSum reformulated extraction as semantic text matching, and long-document extractive summarization models that combine global and local context outperform older baselines on datasets such as PubMed and arXiv. The tradeoff is training and inference cost; the benefit is usually better salience ranking than pure similarity heuristics. If your hard constraint is CPU-only and low-latency indexing, I would still start with the unsupervised hybrid described above before moving to a supervised salience model. 

## How entities should be represented

For your third question, I would **not** stop at a flat entity list, but I also would **not** jump immediately to a full knowledge graph. The best default is a middle ground: a lightweight **entity memory layer** consisting of a normalized entity table, mention spans, chunk and sentence postings, optional entity linking IDs, and a simple co-occurrence graph. That gives you almost all of the engineering value of entity grounding without paying the full cost and fragility of relation extraction over every document. spaCy already supports fast NER, rule-based entity enrichment through `EntityRuler`, and entity linking to a knowledge base. 

In practical terms, every entity should have at least: a canonical form, aliases, type, linked ID if available, numeric attributes if relevant, and postings to the sentences and chunks where it appears. For summarization, that is much more useful than a raw entity list because it preserves **provenance**. It lets you answer questions like “which chunks mention this entity?”, “which salient sentences compare entity A and entity B?”, and “which numbers are attached to this entity via the dependency parse?” spaCy’s documentation explicitly shows how dependency parsing can be combined with recognized entities such as `MONEY` spans to extract the noun phrase they refer to, which is exactly the kind of lightweight factual grounding you want for support summaries. 

For clustering, use entity information as **one factor**, not the whole graph. This aligns with your β term. The good use of entities here is to strengthen otherwise weak semantic links—for example, because two chunks talk about the same chemical, person, court case, or metric but use different wording. A lightweight entity overlap or entity-link overlap score is enough. The mistake would be to force chunks into the same topic only because they share common, high-frequency entities. That is why I would normalize entity contribution by inverse document frequency or by entity centrality within the current document set. The inference here is directly motivated by work showing both the value and the difficulty of extracting compact, salient entity-relation structures from long documents. 

The most relevant paper to your design choice is **E²GraphRAG**. Its reported contribution is almost exactly the hybrid architecture you are converging toward: a **hierarchical tree** obtained by recursively merging and summarizing chunks, plus a **concise entity graph** to integrate fine-grained knowledge scattered across chunks. That is a strong signal that your instinct—tree for multi-granularity summarization, graph for fine-grained recall—is architecturally sound. It also suggests that you do not need to choose between tree and graph; you should use both, but keep the graph lightweight. 

A full knowledge graph becomes worth the cost only when the downstream task truly needs **typed relations and multi-hop reasoning**. There is real evidence that graph-centric models can help multi-document scientific summarization and long-document knowledge extraction, but those papers also underline that salient-graph extraction is itself a hard problem. The 2020 summary-KG paper explicitly frames compact graph extraction from long documents as important but challenging, while more recent KG-based summarization work shows benefits in relation-heavy domains like scientific summarization. My recommendation is therefore: start with a lightweight entity memory and co-occurrence graph, and promote it to a richer KG only if your users need relation-centric retrieval or domain-specific reasoning. 

One more caution is important. Recent CoKG work for multi-document summarization argues that aggressively filtering entities can lose information because some entities that look minor locally may still matter globally. That fits your own concern about contrastive or minority evidence. So entity pruning should be conservative. Keep the full postings index, then build smaller prompt-time entity subsets rather than permanently deleting entities early in the pipeline. 

## Structure-aware segmentation and chunking

If you want paragraph boundaries, header lists, and item lists **without** LLMs, the best principle is simple: **use document structure before semantic segmentation whenever structure is available**. A paper on segmentation of marked-up text explicitly notes that paragraphs, headings, and list items can inform sentence segmentation, and work on hierarchical document structure parsing shows that document structure and layout information is a rich source for downstream NLP. In other words, if your source format preserves structure—HTML, XML, Markdown, DOCX, PDF layout metadata—do not throw it away and then try to infer it from plain text later. 

For born-digital PDFs or scanned pages, layout analysis is the right non-LLM helper. LayoutParser is specifically built for document image analysis and layout detection, and its stated goal is to make document structure extraction practical and reusable. That does not solve semantic chunking by itself, but it gives you very strong **hard boundaries**: title blocks, section headers, list regions, tables, captions, and paragraphs. Those boundaries should constrain your later semantic merging so that a chunk does not casually cross a heading or jump into a bullet list that should have been kept separate. 

If you only have plain text, then use a **block-first pipeline**. First identify candidate blocks from newline patterns, indentation, bullets, numbering, casing, and short-line behavior. Then run a light header/list classifier or deterministic rules on those blocks. Older work on scientific documents used block-level classifiers to distinguish section headers from non-headers after converting documents into roughly paragraph-level text blocks. For topical boundaries inside those blocks, classical segmentation methods like TextTiling and C99 remain useful, especially when combined with modern embeddings rather than purely lexical similarity. 

The chunking policy I would recommend is therefore a **hard-boundary plus soft-boundary** design. Hard boundaries come from layout and structure: headers, numbered items, bullet lists, paragraph breaks, table boundaries, figure captions. Soft boundaries come from topic-shift signals: dips in adjacent-window similarity, C99-style ranked local similarity, and topic changes detected in the semantic graph. The final chunker should merge material only inside compatible hard-boundary regions and only until a semantic or size threshold is reached. This is the cleanest way to stay structure-aware without needing LLM-based segmentation. 

This is also where your three-sentence windows fit best. Use them to smooth the local similarity curve and detect **where** boundaries should be, but once the boundaries are chosen, generate chunks that respect paragraph and list structure. That gives you the best of both worlds: the locality benefits of semantic chunking and the interpretability of document structure. Recent work on long-form faithfulness also suggests that **natural document boundaries** are preferable to arbitrary fixed chunking when assessing long summaries, which reinforces the general principle that structure matters. 

## Efficiency, token budget, and evaluation

Your design can be efficient, but only if you are disciplined about what reaches the LLM. The cheap part of your system is the index: sentence splitting, embedding computation, sparse graph construction, Leiden clustering, spaCy NER, rule-based numeric extraction, and paraphrase-based deduplication are all compatible with a CPU-first pipeline. Sentence-Transformers is designed for efficient embedding inference, fast similarity computation, clustering, semantic search, and paraphrase mining, and spaCy is explicitly optimized for production NLP with fast components and rule-based augmentations. 

The token-intensive part would be the **topic summarization prompt** if you pass redundant evidence. If the prompt contains concatenated chunks, then repeats the same content as salient sentences, then repeats entities again in list form, you are double- or triple-paying for already-available evidence. Recent context-aware hierarchical merging results strongly suggest that what matters is not flooding the model with duplicated text, but giving it **relevant support evidence** alongside the summarization context. So the most token-efficient design is usually one of these two: either pass chunk text once plus compact source-linked support sentences, or pass chunk summaries plus a compact evidence pack of original source sentences for grounding. The wrong pattern is “full chunk text + all salient sentences copied from those chunks + all entities copied from those sentences.” 

This is where your upper-layer graph design becomes crucial. Recent work on positional bias found that hierarchical merging and incremental updating can both hurt faithfulness if structure changes cause the model to miss relevant context. That means your multi-layer graph should **not** become a pure summary-of-summaries tower. Every higher layer should inherit not just text and embeddings, but also a **downsampled support set of original evidence sentences**. Your idea that higher-layer salient sentences should remain sourced from child chunks, not re-generated by the LLM, is exactly the right instinct. 

For evaluation, I would benchmark three things separately. First, evaluate segmentation quality by checking whether boundaries align with headings, paragraphs, and topic changes. Second, evaluate the extract-support module by measuring redundancy, coverage, and whether support sentences preserve minority or contrastive content. Third, evaluate final summaries on both content and faithfulness. For faithfulness, QAFactEval is a strong automated baseline, FActScore is useful when you want fact-level precision on long-form output, and recent ACL work on long-document factual consistency argues for **fine-grained continuous scoring** rather than only binary summary labels. 

So the direct answers to your three questions are these. **Does the current flow look good, efficient, and not overly token-intensive?** Yes, provided you keep a sparse evidence graph, use structure-aware chunking, and avoid redundant prompt packing. **What is the best way to extract salient units without LLMs?** A hybrid of embedding-based centrality, topic relevance, entity/number bonuses, contrast bonuses, and diversity-aware selection with MMR or submodular greedy ranking is the best cost-quality tradeoff; a trained extractive reranker is the next step if you can afford it. **What should you do with entities?** Use a lightweight entity memory and co-occurrence graph linked back to chunks and sentences; do not settle for a bare list, and do not build a full KG by default unless the domain truly needs relation-centric reasoning.

## 1. Updated assessment of the architecture

With the Vietnamese and training-free constraints, I think the architecture is technically sound and research-worthy:

[
\text{structured input}
\rightarrow \text{semantic chunks}
\rightarrow \text{source evidence extraction}
\rightarrow \text{sparse chunk graph}
\rightarrow \text{Leiden topics}
\rightarrow \text{Extract-Support summarization}
\rightarrow \text{recursive topic graph}
]

Its strongest contribution is not simply “Leiden plus summarization.” It is the combination of:

* structure-aware semantic segmentation;
* source-only evidence propagation across hierarchical levels;
* graph-based topic formation;
* lightweight Vietnamese entity processing;
* selectable summary granularity without retraining.

The main remaining risks are:

1. redundant overlapping tri-sentence evidence;
2. communities that exceed the LLM token budget;
3. information drift when higher-level clustering uses only generated-summary embeddings;
4. entity noise and over-aggressive entity deduplication.

All four can be handled without changing the core design.

### A subtle LangChain implementation issue

LangChain’s `SemanticChunker` does use three-sentence contexts when `buffer_size=1`, but internally these are **overlapping centered windows**:

[
u_i=(s_{i-1},s_i,s_{i+1})
]

They are embedded to calculate boundary distances. The returned chunks, however, are reconstructed by joining the original sentences—not by returning the tri-sentence windows as durable objects. Therefore, you need to materialize and store your own `TriSentenceUnit` records before invoking or adapting the chunker. ([GitHub][1])

I would keep both levels:

```text
Sentence:
    sentence_id
    text
    paragraph_id

TriSentenceUnit:
    unit_id
    sentence_ids
    text
    embedding
    center_sentence_id
```

This preserves exactly the source evidence lineage you want.

Because consecutive windows share two of three sentences, selected evidence should be compacted before prompting. For example, selecting units `[s1,s2,s3]` and `[s2,s3,s4]` should produce one prompt span `[s1,s2,s3,s4]`, while retaining both unit IDs internally.

## 2. Best salient-unit extraction approach

For your constraints, my recommendation is:

> **Multilingual reranker for candidate relevance, followed by contrast-aware MMR for final evidence selection.**

Do not use the reranker alone, and do not use plain MMR alone.

### Why not just chunk–unit cosine similarity?

Ranking each tri-unit by

[
\cos(e_{\text{unit}},e_{\text{chunk}})
]

is a useful baseline, but it favors central, repeated statements. It can miss:

* minority viewpoints;
* exceptions;
* contradictory numbers;
* conclusions that are locally important but not close to the global centroid.

PACSUM improves over simple centroid similarity by using graph centrality and positional relationships, and it was designed as an unsupervised method for settings where training data is unavailable across domains and languages. It is a strong baseline, especially if you replace its original encoder with BGE-M3. ([arXiv][2])

However, for the primary method, a pretrained reranker gives you a better semantic relevance signal.

### Recommended reranker

`BAAI/bge-reranker-v2-m3` is a practical starting point. It is a multilingual, approximately 0.6B-parameter cross-encoder and is directly usable through `FlagEmbedding`. Unlike an embedding model, it scores a query–passage pair jointly. ([Hugging Face][3])

BGE-M3 itself supports more than 100 languages and inputs ranging from sentences to documents up to 8,192 tokens, making it appropriate for your Vietnamese chunk and unit representations. ([arXiv][4])

A Vietnamese-specific alternative for an ablation is **ViRanker**, which is built from BGE-M3 and trained specifically for Vietnamese reranking. However, both BGE rerankers and ViRanker are retrieval models, not summarization salience models, so neither should be treated as a complete extractor. ([arXiv][5])

### Constructing the reranker query

Do not use the entire 1,500-token chunk as the query. That is expensive and may truncate important content. Build a compact deterministic pseudo-query:

```text
<header or paragraph title>
<top keyphrases>
<important entities>
<important numerical concepts>
```

For example:

```text
Chủ đề: Chính sách học phí đại học.
Từ khóa: học phí, sinh viên, ngân sách, miễn giảm.
Thực thể: Bộ Giáo dục, Đại học Quốc gia.
Cần tìm: sự kiện, kết quả, số liệu, nguyên nhân và ý kiến đối lập quan trọng.
```

The keyphrases can come from YAKE, TF-IDF or KeyBERT with BGE-M3. No LLM is required.

### Suggested candidate score

For each tri-unit (u_i):

[
R(u_i)=
w_rR_{\text{reranker}}
+w_c\cos(e_i,e_{\text{chunk}})
+w_eE_i
+w_fF_i
+w_sS_i
+w_xX_i
]

where:

* (E_i): entity coverage;
* (F_i): dates, quantities, percentages and other factual anchors;
* (S_i): structural importance, such as proximity to a heading or paragraph lead;
* (X_i): contrast/concession indicators.

An initial configuration could be:

[
(w_r,w_c,w_e,w_f,w_s,w_x)
=(0.40,0.25,0.12,0.10,0.08,0.05)
]

These values should be treated as an ablation starting point, not fixed theory.

### Contrast-aware MMR

Standard MMR selects:

[
u^*=\arg\max_{u\notin A}
\left[
\lambda R(u)
-(1-\lambda)\max_{v\in A}\cos(e_u,e_v)
\right]
]

LangChain currently contains a usable `maximal_marginal_relevance` implementation, so you do not need to implement the basic algorithm from scratch. ([GitHub][6])

The problem is that a contradiction often has high semantic similarity:

* “Doanh thu tăng 20%.”
* “Doanh thu không tăng 20% mà giảm 5%.”

Ordinary MMR may remove one as redundant. Modify the redundancy term:

[
D(u,v)=
\cos(e_u,e_v)\cdot
\left(1-C(u,v)\right)
]

where (C(u,v)) is a contrast likelihood. It can be estimated training-free from:

* Vietnamese negation: `không`, `chưa`, `chẳng`, `không phải`;
* contrast markers: `nhưng`, `tuy nhiên`, `trái lại`, `ngược lại`, `trong khi`, `mặc dù`, `dẫu vậy`, `song`;
* same entity with different numerical values;
* same entity with opposite sentiment or predicate polarity.

You can additionally impose a **minority-topic rescue constraint**: cluster the tri-units inside the chunk into a few small semantic groups and retain at least one high-scoring unit from each meaningful group when the evidence budget permits. This is more reliable for preserving opposing views than simply lowering the MMR redundancy penalty.

### Recommended extraction sequence

1. Remove exact and source-overlap duplicates.
2. Score all tri-units with the reranker and inexpensive features.
3. Keep the top (m), perhaps 10–15 candidates.
4. Apply contrast-aware MMR under a token budget.
5. Ensure coverage of minority subclusters.
6. Compact overlapping selected windows for the prompt.
7. Retain original unit IDs and sentence IDs as provenance.

Use a **token budget**, rather than a fixed `top_k`. For example:

[
B_{\text{evidence}}
=\min(400,;0.20\times\text{chunk tokens})
]

for a 1,000–1,500-token chunk.

### Comparison with previous extractors

| Method                        | Suitability here                                                                     |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Chunk-centroid cosine         | Good minimum baseline, insufficient diversity                                        |
| PACSUM with BGE-M3            | Best simple training-free baseline                                                   |
| LexRank/TextRank              | Useful baseline, less semantically precise                                           |
| MemSum checkpoint             | Strong history-aware extraction, but trained mainly on English long-document corpora |
| MatchSum                      | Good summary-level scoring, but candidate combination becomes expensive              |
| Reranker + contrast-aware MMR | Best practical primary method                                                        |

MemSum explicitly conditions each selection on content, global context and already selected sentences, which helps reduce redundancy, but its existing training and domain assumptions make it better as an experimental baseline than the central Vietnamese method. ([arXiv][7]) MatchSum scores whole candidate summaries against the source, but candidate enumeration is awkward when each chunk contains many overlapping tri-units. ([arXiv][8])

## 3. kNN graph plus Leiden

D2CS is indeed the closest direct precedent for this component. Its initial pipeline computes distances between documents from their sentence-embedding distributions, constructs a kNN graph, and applies graph clustering; Leiden is the community-detection algorithm used in the framework. Its later LLM-guided edge refinement is not suitable for your low-LLM objective, but the initial graph construction is directly reusable. 

Your method differs meaningfully from D2CS:

* D2CS nodes are documents; yours are structure-aware chunks.
* D2CS mainly clusters a corpus once; yours recursively builds abstraction levels.
* D2CS uses LLM feedback to revise clustering; yours uses source structure and entities.
* You preserve source support evidence through the hierarchy.

Vec2GC is another relevant precedent: it converts learned text representations into a weighted graph and then applies community detection, including hierarchical clustering. ([arXiv][9])

### Recommended sparse graph

Do not form kNN from the final combined score alone. First construct a union of candidate edge types:

[
E =
E_{\text{semantic-kNN}}
\cup E_{\text{local}}
\cup E_{\text{entity}}
]

where:

* `semantic-kNN`: BGE-M3 nearest neighbours;
* `local`: adjacent or near-adjacent chunks from the same document;
* `entity`: chunks sharing sufficiently informative entities.

Then compute the final weight only for candidate edges:

[
w_{ij}
=\alpha s_{\text{position}}(i,j)
+\beta s_{\text{entity}}(i,j)
+\gamma s_{\text{content}}(i,j)
]

A reasonable starting point is:

[
(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)=(0.10,0.20,0.70)
]

with ablations around it.

For position:

[
s_{\text{position}}(i,j)=
\begin{cases}
\exp(-|p_i-p_j|/\tau), & d_i=d_j\
0, & d_i\neq d_j
\end{cases}
]

Position must be document-relative. Chunk 4 in article A is not “near” chunk 5 in article B. For news collections, timestamp similarity should be a separate signal rather than being mixed into document position.

For entity similarity, use entity TF-IDF cosine or weighted Jaccard rather than plain overlap:

[
x_{ie}
=\operatorname{tf}(e,i)
\log\frac{N+1}{\operatorname{df}(e)+1}
]

[
s_{\text{entity}}(i,j)
=\cos(x_i,x_j)
]

This prevents a common entity such as “Việt Nam” or “Chính phủ” from connecting nearly everything.

Since you already store tri-unit embeddings, an interesting D2CS-inspired ablation is:

[
s_{\text{content}}
=0.75\cos(e_{\text{chunk},i},e_{\text{chunk},j})
+0.25s_{\text{distribution}}(U_i,U_j)
]

where (s_{\text{distribution}}) compares the sets of tri-unit embeddings. Calculate this expensive second term only for candidate kNN edges.

### Controlling community size

Leiden optimizes graph structure, not your LLM token limit. Therefore every community should satisfy:

[
\sum_{c_i\in T_j}\operatorname{tokens}(c_i)
\le B_{\text{topic}}
]

When a community exceeds the budget:

1. rerun Leiden on that induced subgraph with higher resolution;
2. if it remains oversized, split using the strongest internal semantic boundary;
3. never arbitrarily truncate chunks.

This allows the model to remain scalable even when one broad subject dominates the document.

## 4. Best entity design

The best design is neither a flat list nor a full relation-heavy knowledge graph.

Use a **lightweight heterogeneous evidence graph with bidirectional entity postings**:

```text
Entity <──mentions──> TriSentenceUnit
Entity <──appears_in──> Chunk
Chunk  <──belongs_to──> Topic
Topic  <──supported_by──> TriSentenceUnit
Chunk  <──similar_to──> Chunk
```

This is close to the efficient part of E²GraphRAG. E²GraphRAG constructs a summary tree, extracts entities from chunks using spaCy, and creates bidirectional entity–chunk indexes for fast local and global retrieval. ([arXiv][10])

For your summarization framework, the entity layer should have three roles:

1. contribute to chunk edge weights;
2. retrieve source evidence for an entity or pair of entities;
3. provide a compact factual ledger to the summarization prompt.

A full KG with automatically extracted typed relations is not necessary for the first version. Relation extraction would add another noisy model and may create false edges. Your source tri-units already contain the actual relations in text form.

### Vietnamese NER

For Vietnamese, I would prefer **PhoNLP or VnCoreNLP over a generic spaCy pipeline**.

VnCoreNLP provides Vietnamese word segmentation, POS tagging, NER and dependency parsing in a fast Java pipeline. ([ACL Anthology][11]) PhoNLP jointly handles POS tagging, NER and dependency parsing using a Vietnamese pretrained model and reported stronger benchmark results than independent PhoBERT task models. ([ACL Anthology][12])

A practical choice is:

* VnCoreNLP when throughput and CPU efficiency dominate;
* PhoNLP when NER quality is more important;
* regex/rule components for dates, money, percentages, measurements, legal references and social-media handles.

### Do not treat numbers as ordinary entities

Store numerical facts separately:

```text
NumericFact:
    value
    normalized_value
    unit
    associated_entity_ids
    time_expression
    predicate_hint
    source_unit_id
```

For example:

```text
value = 20
unit = "%"
entity = "doanh thu"
predicate = "tăng"
time = "quý II/2025"
```

This makes it possible to detect conflicting evidence such as 20% versus 12%, and it helps the support selector preserve both versions instead of treating them as duplicates.

### Entity canonicalization

Use this order:

1. Unicode and whitespace normalization;
2. exact normalized-string matching;
3. acronym and abbreviation rules;
4. type-constrained fuzzy matching;
5. type-constrained embedding similarity only as a fallback.

Do not deduplicate entities using embeddings alone. Semantically close names may represent different organizations or people.

Each canonical entity should store:

```text
entity_id
canonical_name
display_name
entity_type
aliases
mention_unit_ids
mention_chunk_ids
document_frequency
confidence
```

### LLM-generated entities

I would change one part of your proposed topic node.

Do **not** directly union LLM-generated entities into the trusted entity set. Store them as:

```text
generated_entity_candidates
```

Then promote a candidate only if it can be matched to:

* a source-extracted entity;
* a selected support unit;
* or an explicit source string span.

Otherwise, a single hallucinated entity can propagate upward, affect clustering and be repeated in all higher summaries.

Source entities should be the authoritative graph. LLM entities can improve aliases or discover missed candidates, but they must be grounded before becoming graph nodes.

## 5. Recommended higher-level topic representation

Do not represent a topic using only the embedding of its generated summary. Generated summaries may omit a secondary viewpoint, causing that viewpoint to disappear from the next Leiden layer.

Store two embeddings:

```text
summary_embedding = embed(generated_summary)

source_embedding =
    weighted_mean(child_chunk_embeddings,
                  weights=child_salience_or_coverage)
```

Then use:

[
s_{\text{topic-content}}(i,j)
=============================

\eta\cos(e^{\text{summary}}_i,e^{\text{summary}}_j)
+(1-\eta)\cos(e^{\text{source}}_i,e^{\text{source}}_j)
]

Starting with (\eta=0.5) is reasonable.

A topic node could therefore contain:

```text
TopicNode:
    topic_id
    level
    summary_text
    summary_embedding
    source_embedding
    child_ids
    source_document_ids
    entity_statistics
    numeric_fact_ids
    support_unit_ids
    token_count
    provenance
```

At every new level:

1. cluster topic nodes;
2. collect child summaries;
3. form the candidate support pool from the union of child source supports;
4. rerank and reselect a smaller support subset;
5. summarize using child summaries plus selected source evidence;
6. aggregate source-derived entities;
7. compute both summary and source embeddings.

## Final recommendation

The strongest concrete version of your system is:

```text
Regex/structure-aware sentence splitting
    ↓
Persistent overlapping tri-sentence source units
    ↓
Paragraph-constrained SemanticChunker
    ↓
1,000–1,500-token chunks with hard maximum
    ↓
PhoNLP/VnCoreNLP + numeric extraction
    ↓
BGE-M3 embeddings
    ↓
BGE multilingual reranker + contrast-aware MMR
    ↓
Sparse semantic-kNN + adjacency + entity graph
    ↓
Leiden with token-bounded communities
    ↓
Topic summarization:
    child text/summaries
    + source-only support evidence
    + compact entity/numeric ledger
    + structured reasoning prompt
    ↓
Dual topic embeddings
    ↓
Repeat
```

For the paper, I would position **PACSUM with BGE-M3** as the strong training-free extraction baseline and **reranker + contrast-aware MMR** as your proposed extractor. For entities, the primary system should use the lightweight entity–chunk–evidence graph; a flat list and a richer relation KG can be clean ablations.

## 1. PACSUM + BGE-M3 versus reranker + contrast-aware MMR

There is no direct benchmark comparing these exact configurations for Vietnamese tri-sentence extraction, so the ranking below is an informed expectation rather than an established result.

### Expected comparison

| Aspect                           | PACSUM + BGE-M3                      | Reranker + contrast-aware MMR              |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------ |
| Central-topic coverage           | Strong                               | Strong if the pseudo-query is good         |
| Minority/contrastive ideas       | Weak–moderate                        | Stronger                                   |
| Redundancy reduction             | Moderate                             | Strong                                     |
| Unexpected important facts       | Better                               | May miss them if absent from query         |
| Numerical/contradictory evidence | Not handled explicitly               | Can be protected explicitly                |
| Incremental CPU/GPU cost         | Very low after embeddings            | Much higher                                |
| Implementation complexity        | Low                                  | Moderate                                   |
| Sensitivity to parameters        | Similarity threshold/graph settings  | Query, reranker, (\lambda), contrast rules |
| Training-free                    | Yes                                  | Yes, using pretrained models               |
| Best role                        | Fast baseline or candidate generator | Final evidence selector                    |

### PACSUM + BGE-M3

PACSUM is fundamentally a graph-centrality method: tri-units become nodes, semantic similarity defines weighted edges, and highly central units are ranked as salient. More recent work characterizes PACSUM and TextRank as centrality-based extractors and notes that such systems often need positional priors to obtain strong results. ([arXiv][1])

Replacing PACSUM’s original sentence representation with BGE-M3 should improve multilingual semantic similarity. BGE-M3 supports more than 100 languages and was designed for sentence-to-long-document representations and multilingual retrieval. ([arXiv][2])

The computational profile is excellent. Assuming embeddings are already generated for your graph:

[
\text{PACSUM cost}\approx O(n^2d)
]

for (n) tri-units and embedding dimension (d). At chunk level, (n) will usually be small enough that this is negligible compared with generating the embeddings.

The main weakness is conceptual: **centrality is not the same as summarization importance**. PACSUM tends to favor information that resembles many other units. That is useful for identifying the dominant topic, but potentially harmful for:

* a minority opinion appearing once;
* a sentence beginning with “however” that contradicts the dominant account;
* an exceptional case;
* a decisive but unique numerical result;
* the final conclusion of a long argument.

The overlapping tri-sentences introduce an additional issue. Suppose you have:

[
u_i=(s_i,s_{i+1},s_{i+2})
]

and

[
u_{i+1}=(s_{i+1},s_{i+2},s_{i+3})
]

Their similarity will be high partly because two-thirds of their source sentences are identical. PACSUM may consequently measure **window overlap and local density**, rather than actual document-wide salience.

You would need to compensate by either:

[
\operatorname{sim}'(u_i,u_j)
============================

\operatorname{sim}(u_i,u_j)
\left(1-\operatorname{sourceOverlap}(u_i,u_j)\right)
]

or by excluding immediately overlapping windows from the PACSUM graph.

### Reranker + contrast-aware MMR

A cross-encoder reranker jointly processes the pseudo-query and candidate tri-unit, producing a relevance score rather than independently generated embeddings. The BGE model card describes `bge-reranker-v2-m3` as a multilingual reranker based on BGE-M3; it has approximately 0.6B parameters. ([Hugging Face][3])

It should generally produce more precise relevance judgments than embedding cosine because it can model token-level interactions between:

```text
topic description / keyphrases / entities
```

and:

```text
candidate tri-sentence
```

However, that performance depends heavily on the pseudo-query. If the query emphasizes only the dominant topic, the reranker can confidently suppress an important but unexpected idea.

That is why MMR is needed:

[
u^*=
\arg\max_{u\notin A}
\left[
\lambda R(u)
-(1-\lambda)\max_{v\in A}D(u,v)
\right]
]

Here, (R(u)) is reranker relevance, while (D(u,v)) penalizes redundancy.

Your contrast-aware version should reduce the redundancy penalty when two similar units disagree:

[
D(u,v)
======

\cos(e_u,e_v)
\left(1-C(u,v)\right)
]

For example, when two units:

* mention the same entity;
* discuss the same measurement;
* but contain different values, polarity or contrast markers,

then (C(u,v)) becomes high and both units can survive selection.

This is much better aligned with your explicit requirement to retain contrasting information.

### Computational cost

Suppose one chunk contains 30 tri-units and you select 5.

For PACSUM after embeddings:

* approximately (30^2=900) cosine calculations;
* no additional transformer inference.

For the reranker:

* 30 query–unit cross-encoder evaluations;
* batchable, but each evaluation runs the 0.6B reranker;
* MMR itself adds only roughly (30\times5) embedding comparisons.

Therefore, the reranker—not MMR—is the expensive component. The BGE documentation calls the v2-M3 reranker lightweight relative to larger rerankers, but it remains significantly more expensive than cosine calculations over cached embeddings. ([Hugging Face][3])

On GPU, this is likely acceptable because your chunks contain relatively few tri-units and reranking can be batched. On CPU, applying it to every unit of every very long document may become a noticeable preprocessing bottleneck.

## Recommended design: PACSUM prefilter + reranker + MMR

I would not choose only one. The strongest practical pipeline is:

[
\text{BGE-M3 embeddings}
\rightarrow
\text{modified PACSUM candidate ranking}
\rightarrow
\text{reranker}
\rightarrow
\text{contrast-aware MMR}
]

For each chunk:

1. PACSUM ranks all (n) tri-units.
2. Preserve special units automatically:

   * contrast markers;
   * numerical claims;
   * paragraph-leading or concluding units;
   * rare but important entities.
3. Send only the top (m) candidates to the reranker, such as:

[
m=\min(15,\lceil 0.5n\rceil)
]

4. Use contrast-aware MMR to choose the final evidence budget.
5. Merge selected overlapping windows into source spans before inserting them into the LLM prompt.

This gives most of the expected quality of full reranking while reducing the number of cross-encoder calls.

My expected quality ranking is:

[
\boxed{
\text{PACSUM-prefilter + reranker + contrast-MMR}

>

\text{reranker + contrast-MMR}

>

\text{PACSUM + BGE-M3}

>

\text{centroid cosine top-k}
}
]

But there is one important caveat: an overly aggressive PACSUM prefilter could discard the same minority evidence you want to protect. Therefore, the prefilter must include “protected candidates” based on contrast, numbers and rare entities.

### Suitable paper setup

Use these as separate experimental systems:

* **E1:** BGE-M3 chunk-centroid top-(k)
* **E2:** PACSUM with BGE-M3
* **E3:** reranker top-(k)
* **E4:** reranker + standard MMR
* **E5:** reranker + contrast-aware MMR
* **E6:** modified PACSUM prefilter + reranker + contrast-aware MMR

E2 is a strong training-free baseline. E5 is your main extraction contribution. E6 is the efficiency-oriented version.

## 2. PhoNLP versus spaCy for Vietnamese entities

This comparison is slightly uneven because **PhoNLP is a pretrained Vietnamese model**, whereas spaCy currently provides Vietnamese language/tokenization support but no official pretrained Vietnamese pipeline. spaCy’s documentation lists Vietnamese with “none yet” under trained pipelines; a blank Vietnamese spaCy pipeline contains language/tokenizer configuration but no pretrained NER component. ([spaCy][4])

Consequently:

* `PhoNLP` versus `spacy.blank("vi")` is not a fair learned-NER comparison.
* `spacy.blank("vi")` cannot automatically recognize normal named entities unless you add rules or train/install another model.
* A custom Vietnamese transformer inside spaCy could be competitive, but it would no longer be the simple training-free spaCy option.

### Expected comparison

| Aspect                              | PhoNLP                | spaCy Vietnamese + rules           |
| ----------------------------------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Pretrained Vietnamese NER           | Yes                   | No official pipeline               |
| Person/organization/location recall | Much better           | Only entities covered by rules     |
| Numerical extraction                | Needs regex extension | Strong with Matcher/regex          |
| Social-media handles/hashtags       | Needs rules           | Easy with tokenizer/rules          |
| Domain-specific entities            | Limited without rules | Easy to encode explicitly          |
| CPU throughput                      | Moderate              | Very high for rules                |
| GPU usefulness                      | High                  | Usually unnecessary for rules      |
| Memory                              | PhoBERT-sized model   | Small for blank pipeline/rules     |
| POS/dependency information          | Included              | Not pretrained for Vietnamese      |
| Integration flexibility             | Moderate              | Excellent                          |
| Training-free suitability           | Yes                   | Yes for rules, not statistical NER |

### PhoNLP quality and speed

PhoNLP uses PhoBERT-base as its contextual encoder, followed by task-specific layers for POS tagging, CRF-based NER and dependency parsing. ([arXiv][5])

Its paper reports Vietnamese NER F1 of:

[
94.51
]

on the VLSP 2016 NER test setup. The authors also reported approximately:

* 15 sentences/second on an Intel Core i5-8265U CPU;
* 129 sentences/second on an RTX 2080 Ti;
* average sentence length of 23 tokens;
* batch size 8.

These figures come from the original paper’s hardware and should not be interpreted as modern-machine benchmarks, but they establish that CPU inference is viable while GPU batching is substantially faster. ([arXiv][5])

PhoNLP is likely to be markedly better for normal Vietnamese entities such as people, organizations and locations because it is explicitly pretrained and evaluated for Vietnamese NER. ([arXiv][6])

Its weaknesses for your application are:

* social-media and informal text may differ from the VLSP news-style distribution;
* entity types are comparatively limited;
* dates, percentages, currency values and measurements should not rely on ordinary NER;
* long chunks must still be processed sentence by sentence;
* PhoNLP expects Vietnamese word-segmented forms internally, which may complicate character-span alignment with your original source.

### spaCy performance

A blank Vietnamese spaCy pipeline is mainly useful as:

* a tokenizer;
* a pipeline framework;
* `Matcher`, `PhraseMatcher` or `EntityRuler`;
* an interface for custom components.

The official documentation states that languages without a trained pipeline have no pretrained components, and Vietnamese currently belongs to that category. ([spaCy][4])

For rule-based entities, spaCy should be substantially faster than PhoNLP because it does not need to execute a BERT encoder. However, its quality is determined entirely by your patterns and dictionaries.

It will work especially well for highly regular facts:


# Proposed training-free Vietnamese long-/multi-document summarization pipeline

The framework is a **structure-aware hierarchical summarization graph**. It uses pretrained encoders and lightweight Vietnamese NLP, while reserving LLM calls for abstractive summarization rather than chunk-level extraction.

The central flow is:

```text
Structured Vietnamese documents
        ↓
Rule-based sentence and paragraph detection
        ↓
Overlapping tri-sentence source units
        ↓
Paragraph-aware semantic chunking
        ↓
BGE-M3 embeddings + Underthesea entities/numerical facts
        ↓
Source-only salient evidence extraction
        ↓
Sparse weighted chunk graph
        ↓
Leiden topic clustering
        ↓
Extract-Support topic summarization
        ↓
Topic nodes
        ↓
Repeat clustering and summarization hierarchically
```

The resulting hierarchy supports summaries at different detail levels while retaining traceability to the original source.

---

## 1. Document preprocessing

### Input

The expected input is somewhat structured Vietnamese text:

* news articles;
* social-media posts;
* reports and documents;
* multi-document collections;
* other texts with meaningful order and paragraph structure.

Each document should retain metadata such as:

```python
Document:
    document_id
    text
    source
    timestamp
    title
    document_order
```

Metadata is useful for ordering, graph construction and provenance, but the core framework should work even when only `document_id` and `text` are present.

### Text normalization

Perform only conservative normalization:

* normalize Unicode;
* standardize whitespace;
* remove repeated page headers or footers when detectable;
* preserve paragraph breaks;
* preserve bullets and heading markers;
* preserve original character offsets.

Do not aggressively lowercase or remove punctuation because these signals are important for sentence splitting, entity recognition and numerical extraction.

---

## 2. Sentence and paragraph structure

### Sentence splitting

Use a deterministic or rule-based Vietnamese sentence splitter.

Every sentence should preserve:

```python
Sentence:
    sentence_id
    document_id
    paragraph_id
    text
    char_start
    char_end
    sentence_index
```

The splitter may occasionally over-segment constructions such as:

```text
Nó có hoạt động không? Có.
```

That is why individual sentences are not used as the main evidence unit.

### Paragraph and structural hints

Detect paragraph boundaries using inexpensive rules:

* blank lines;
* heading-like lines;
* bullet and numbered-list markers;
* Markdown/HTML structure, when available;
* indentation or layout metadata, when available;
* individual social-media post boundaries.

These boundaries are **semantic-chunking hints**, not necessarily unconditional hard boundaries.

A paragraph break should increase the likelihood of a chunk boundary, while still permitting adjacent short paragraphs to be merged when semantically coherent.

---

## 3. Tri-sentence source units

Create overlapping windows of three consecutive sentences.

For an interior sentence (s_i):

[
u_i=(s_{i-1},s_i,s_{i+1})
]

Boundary units use the available neighboring sentences.

```python
TriSentenceUnit:
    unit_id
    document_id
    sentence_ids
    paragraph_ids
    text
    center_sentence_index
    char_start
    char_end
    embedding
    entities
    numeric_facts
```

These units have several purposes:

* compensate for sentence-splitter over-segmentation;
* provide meaningful local context;
* act as the atomic source-evidence unit;
* support semantic boundary calculation;
* provide candidates for extractive evidence selection.

All tri-sentence units remain stored, even when an extraction experiment evaluates only a subset of them.

---

## 4. Semantic chunking

Use LangChain Experimental’s `SemanticChunker`, or an adapted equivalent, with BGE-M3 embeddings.

The chunker should use three-sentence local context and account for detected paragraph boundaries.

### Target chunk size

The desired range is:

[
1{,}000\text{–}1{,}500 \text{ LLM tokens}
]

A practical procedure is:

1. detect candidate semantic boundaries;
2. increase the boundary score at paragraph or section transitions;
3. merge undersized adjacent segments when semantically compatible;
4. split segments larger than 1,500 tokens at their strongest internal boundary;
5. avoid splitting a paragraph unless required by the hard token limit.

Each resulting chunk contains references to its source units:

```python
ChunkNode:
    chunk_id
    document_id
    text
    token_count
    unit_ids
    paragraph_ids
    position
    embedding
    entity_ids
    numeric_fact_ids
    salient_unit_ids
```

Compute the chunk embedding directly from its full text using BGE-M3 rather than relying only on the average of its unit embeddings.

---

## 5. Embedding generation

Use **BGE-M3** for:

* tri-sentence embeddings;
* chunk embeddings;
* topic-summary embeddings;
* source-based topic embeddings.

Batch embedding requests and cache results by normalized-text hash.

Recommended storage:

```python
embedding: np.ndarray
embedding_model: "BAAI/bge-m3"
embedding_version: str
```

Normalize embeddings before cosine similarity calculations.

Tri-sentence embeddings are calculated once and reused for:

* semantic chunking;
* salience extraction;
* chunk similarity;
* deduplication and provenance analysis.

---

## 6. Vietnamese entity and numerical extraction

The initial system uses only **NER-2**:

> **Underthesea NER plus rule-based numerical and structured-fact extraction.**

PhoNLP is excluded from the initial implementation and kept as future work.

### Underthesea

Run Underthesea on source sentences or tri-sentence units to identify ordinary named entities such as:

* people;
* organizations;
* locations;
* other entity classes supported by the toolkit.

Run NER over smaller source units rather than entire 1,500-token chunks. This simplifies span alignment and reduces the chance of losing exact source offsets.

### Rule-based extraction

Add regex and normalization rules for:

* dates and time periods;
* currency;
* percentages;
* quantities and measurements;
* phone numbers and identifiers;
* legal or document references;
* URLs;
* hashtags;
* usernames;
* social-media mentions.

Numerical facts should be stored separately from normal named entities:

```python
NumericFact:
    fact_id
    raw_text
    normalized_value
    unit
    associated_entity_ids
    source_unit_id
    predicate_hint
    time_expression
```

Example:

```text
"Doanh thu tăng 20% trong quý II/2025."
```

could produce:

```python
{
    "normalized_value": 20,
    "unit": "%",
    "associated_entity": "doanh thu",
    "predicate_hint": "tăng",
    "time_expression": "quý II/2025"
}
```

### Entity normalization

Use a conservative normalization pipeline:

1. Unicode and whitespace normalization;
2. exact normalized-name matching;
3. abbreviation and acronym rules;
4. type-compatible fuzzy matching.

Do not merge entities only because their embeddings are similar.

Maintain inverted mappings:

```python
entity_to_unit_ids
entity_to_chunk_ids
chunk_to_entity_ids
unit_to_entity_ids
```

This creates a lightweight entity graph without requiring relation extraction or a full knowledge graph.

---

## 7. Salient evidence extraction

The evidence must always originate from source tri-sentence units.

At the chunk level:

[
E_c \subseteq U_c
]

where (U_c) is the set of tri-sentence units belonging to chunk (c).

At higher levels:

[
E_p \subseteq \bigcup_{c\in children(p)} E_c
]

Thus, higher-level nodes only reselect a subset from the source evidence inherited from their children. They never generate new evidence. This follows the Extract-Support idea in *Context-Aware Hierarchical Merging*: previous summaries provide coverage, while source-derived passages are restated alongside them as supporting evidence during merging. 

The initial experiments include only E1, E2a and E2b.

### E1: chunk-centroid cosine ranking

For every tri-sentence unit (u_i) inside chunk (c):

[
score(u_i)=
\cos(e_{u_i}, e_c)
]

Rank units by similarity to the chunk embedding and select the top units under an evidence token budget.

This is:

* simple;
* computationally inexpensive;
* easy to reproduce;
* a suitable minimum baseline.

Selection should use a token budget rather than a fixed count. For example:

[
B_{\text{evidence}}
===================

\min(400,;0.2\times \text{chunk tokens})
]

### E2a: PACSUM with all tri-sentence units

Construct a similarity graph among all tri-sentence units inside the chunk using their BGE-M3 embeddings.

PACSUM assigns higher scores to units that are semantically central while incorporating positional directionality.

The selected units are the highest-ranked units that fit the same evidence budget used by E1.

### E2b: PACSUM with stride-2 tri-sentence units

PACSUM considers only:

[
u_2,u_4,u_6,\ldots
]

This reduces the artificial similarity caused by adjacent overlapping windows.

Adjacent evaluated units now share approximately one source sentence instead of two.

If the original chunk contains (n) units, pairwise graph computation changes approximately from:

[
O(n^2)
]

to:

[
O((n/2)^2)=O(n^2/4)
]

All tri-sentence units remain stored, but only even-indexed units participate in this PACSUM variant.

No protected-candidate logic or contrast-aware postprocessing is included in the initial implementation.

### Evidence compaction

Selected windows may still overlap. Before inserting them into the LLM prompt, merge overlapping selections into continuous source spans.

For example:

```text
[s1, s2, s3]
[s3, s4, s5]
```

becomes:

```text
[s1, s2, s3, s4, s5]
```

Internally, preserve the original unit and sentence IDs.

---

## 8. Sparse chunk-similarity graph

Each chunk is a graph node.

Avoid a fully connected graph. First generate candidate edges using semantic k-nearest neighbours:

[
E_{\text{candidate}}
====================

E_{\text{kNN}}
\cup E_{\text{adjacent}}
]

`E_adjacent` preserves immediate structural continuity even when semantic similarity is relatively low.

A starting value is:

```python
k = 8
```

but this should be tuned according to document size.

### Edge weight

For candidate chunks (i) and (j):

[
w_{ij}
======

\alpha P_{ij}
+
\beta E_{ij}
+
\gamma C_{ij}
]

subject to:

[
\alpha+\beta+\gamma=1,
\qquad
\alpha\leq0.2
]

A reasonable initial setting is:

[
\alpha=0.10,\qquad
\beta=0.20,\qquad
\gamma=0.70
]

where:

* (P_{ij}): positional similarity;
* (E_{ij}): entity similarity;
* (C_{ij}): BGE-M3 chunk-content similarity.

### Positional similarity

Position only applies within the same source document:

[
P_{ij}
======

\begin{cases}
\exp(-|p_i-p_j|/\tau), & d_i=d_j\
0, & d_i\neq d_j
\end{cases}
]

For multi-document inputs, two chunks are not positionally close merely because their chunk indices are similar.

### Entity similarity

Represent each chunk as a sparse entity vector weighted by entity frequency and inverse document frequency.

Then use cosine similarity or weighted Jaccard similarity.

This reduces the effect of globally common entities such as “Việt Nam.”

### Content similarity

Use cosine similarity between normalized BGE-M3 chunk embeddings:

[
C_{ij}
======

\cos(e_i,e_j)
]

---

## 9. Leiden topic clustering

Run Leiden community detection over the sparse weighted graph.

Each resulting community represents a topic:

```python
TopicNode:
    topic_id
    level
    child_ids
    source_document_ids
    summary_text
    summary_embedding
    source_embedding
    entity_ids
    numeric_fact_ids
    salient_unit_ids
    token_count
```

### Token-bounded communities

Leiden does not know the LLM’s context budget.

After clustering, calculate the total input size of each community. When a community is too large:

1. rerun Leiden on the induced community subgraph with higher resolution;
2. repeat until communities fit the configured topic budget;
3. use a semantic boundary split as a deterministic fallback.

No chunk should be silently truncated.

### Ordering within topics

After clustering, restore source order before summarization:

* document order;
* position within document;
* timestamp when processing news or social-media collections.

Clustering determines topic membership, not presentation order.

---

## 10. Topic-level Extract-Support summarization

The LLM is called once for each topic.

At the first hierarchy level, the prompt contains:

1. ordered chunk texts;
2. selected source tri-sentence evidence;
3. normalized entity information;
4. numerical facts;
5. summarization instructions.

At subsequent levels, it contains:

1. child-topic summaries;
2. a reselected subset of inherited source evidence;
3. aggregated source-derived entities and numerical facts;
4. summarization instructions.

The prompt should instruct the model to:

* summarize the main topic comprehensively;
* use evidence to check factual claims;
* preserve disagreement and contrast;
* distinguish confirmed facts from opinions;
* avoid introducing unsupported entities or numbers;
* preserve uncertainty;
* produce a summary at the requested level of detail.

A reasoning-directed prompt inspired by CoTHSSum can guide the model to identify themes, relationships and contrasts before generating the final summary. The visible output need only contain the final structured result rather than the model’s full reasoning trace.

Suggested structured output:

```json
{
  "summary": "...",
  "key_points": ["...", "..."],
  "contrasts": ["..."],
  "mentioned_entity_candidates": ["..."]
}
```

The entity candidates returned by the LLM are not immediately added to the trusted entity graph. They may be stored for analysis but should only be promoted when matched to a source mention.

---

## 11. Topic embeddings and recursive hierarchy

For each topic, compute two embeddings.

### Summary embedding

[
e^{summary}_t
=============

\operatorname{BGE\text{-}M3}(summary_t)
]

This represents the generated abstraction.

### Source embedding

Compute an aggregate from child source embeddings:

[
e^{source}_t
============

\operatorname{Normalize}
\left(
\sum_{c\in children(t)}
\omega_c e^{source}_c
\right)
]

At the first topic level, child source embeddings are chunk embeddings.

Weights may initially be uniform or proportional to chunk token count.

Using both embeddings prevents an omitted secondary detail in the generated summary from disappearing completely at the next clustering level.

For higher-level topic similarity:

[
C^{topic}_{ij}
==============

\eta\cos(e^{summary}_i,e^{summary}_j)
+
(1-\eta)\cos(e^{source}_i,e^{source}_j)
]

with an initial value:

[
\eta=0.5
]

Then repeat:

```text
Topic nodes
    ↓
Sparse kNN graph
    ↓
Weighted edges
    ↓
Leiden communities
    ↓
Reselect source support
    ↓
LLM summarization
    ↓
Higher-level topic nodes
```

The process stops when:

* only one topic remains;
* the desired summary level is reached;
* or the whole current level fits into one final summarization context.

\text{rule-based numerical extraction}
}
]

For a Vietnamese-focused paper, Underthesea remains the primary method. The English route is mainly useful for testing whether the rest of the graph and summarization pipeline generalizes across languages.
